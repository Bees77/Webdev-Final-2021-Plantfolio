Why is Botany important?

Plants make up 82% of our global biomass, and they have more biodiversity than any animal species. Botany is the study of plants, how they grow, and conservation of endangered species. Without plants, everything would die, and with decreasing biodiversity as a result of human activities, we need to learn more about botanical sciences so we can curb our behaviors that damage ecosystems, conserve endangere species, and preserve biodiversity.

How to Care for Plants (or at least most of them)

Basic Needs

Pretty much every botanical organism shares some basic needs, namely water, soil, and sunlight.

Water

Water requirements for plants are typically based on their native climate. While this can be difficult to determine without knowing a plant's species, usually physical features such as succulent or waxy leaves, or brittle or bendy wood, can indicate the water requirements of a plant.

Succulents are technically herbaceous, but store water in their leaves, and usually have thick, fluid filled leaves because of this. They are different from cacti in this way, as cacti store water in their stems, and have adapted their leaves into needles. This is contrary to popular belief that succulents and cacti are the same, and therefore can equally tolerate drought. Both are drought tolerant, but cacti can go much longer without being watered, and are actually more prone to overwatering. Both should be watered using soak and dry method, meaning soaking their soil in water and allowing to dry completely before watering again. Cacti usually don't give much indication of underwatering, other than possible becoming shriveled, but succulents leaves will become brown and crinkly and drop. Both will turn yellow if overwatered, and look as if they are bulging with water. Sometimes this can lead to root rot, so be very careful with watering if you see yellowing around the base of a succulent or cacti stem, And if the stem begins to turn black and drop leaves, it is rotting and needs to be cut off. Tropical succulents can usually take more water, but are still prone to overwatering, so wait antil the soil is barely damp before rewatering.

Herbs need a little more care, and usually need more water. Some woody herbs like rosemary and other shrubs can go a while without watering, and usually indicate dehydration through dry, brittle stems. Herbs like mint, however, need contantly moist soil. Some people will say you can let them dry out before rewatering. Those people are lying. In Texas, plants that usually would need more spaced out watering intervals can go less time before getting dehydrated, especially when grown outdoors. This can lead to leaf burning and infection. Don't let those leaves get all crinkly or else there's no going back.

Sunlight

All plants(as well as some algaes and microorganisms) perform photosynthesis, though depending on native climates they do so to different extents. Plants from shadier, heavily forested regions with thick canopies tend to be rather intolerant of direct sunlight, and have leaves that burn very easily, such as ferns. Plants from hot, dry areas store moisture in their stems and leaves and therefore are more tolerant to direct sunlight. Most plants can only tolerate partial sun, but will droop or become leggy if grown in an area that's to shady.

Soil

CLAY SOIL IS BAD AND YOU SHOULD HATE IT AS MUCH AS I DO. If nothing else, that is what you need to know about soil. Pretty much my whole yard has clay soil and it's a nightmare. This is prone to happen in areas with heavy rainfall, land neglect, and poor drainage (like at the bottom of a hill or slope). IT SUCKS AND KILLS EVERYTHING. It's also why you should always by good quality soil if planning a garden bed. Clay soil is cheap, but it will cost you more in the long run and malnourish your plants.

Why You Should Care

Okay I will admit this started as just rambling because I have an outlet for all my planty interest now, but this project was supposed to be a website with a purpose, and while me sharing all my cool plant pics and babbling about botany seems pretty purposeless, there really is purpose to be foun in cultivating and caring for native and foreign plants. Here's some facts about invasive species, plant conservation, and the role plants play in our ecosystem.

Invasive plants

Invasive plants are a huge issue, especially in Texas. They disturb local ecosystems by competing with native plant populations for resources, genetically evolving more quickly than surrounding organisms, and killing plants that are a part of native animals' diets, which can cause overall population decline in anything that is livng, which is bad. Some invasive plants spread naturally, but a lot are spread by human activity. Rosemary is an example of an invasive herb in Texas, and it should only be grown in pots.

Deforestation

Deforestation is an entirely human activity that causees catastrophic damage to ecosystems and botanical communities.

Irresponsible Imports

A new market for houseplants has arrisen in recent years, and while this is great and benefits both plants and gardeners, and gives people in urban areas access to botany, it has also sparked an illegal trade of exotic plants across oceans and biomes, which can cause environmental havoc. This can bring invasive plants and insects into ecosystems that have no place for them, leading to imbalance of food and water chains and overpopulation, as well as the spreading of pathogens. Make sure you buy your plants from responsible growers, and pay attention to whether a plant on the market is an endangered species.
